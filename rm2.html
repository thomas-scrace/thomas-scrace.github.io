Thoughts on the reMarkable 2

6 Jan 2021

Hardware

The device is very well made and feels great in the hand. It's very thin. It feels like the future. I did not get the case, and I do not recommend getting the case. It feels great in its unprotected form and I don't think it needs much protection.

There is no logo printed on the front of the device, which I like, but it does show the logo on the screen when sleeping or turned off.

It has a single hardware button, which is used for powering on/off and sleeping.

The stylus attaches to the left-hand side of the device using magnets. The magnets are strong enough that you can hold the device up just by the stylus and it will not fall off. It snaps into place nicely without too much faffing.

The stylus does not require charging.

Battery

I have found that the battery depletes quite rapidly under moderately heavy use, which surprised me. If you are taking notes all day and using it for reading too, be prepared to charge it every 2 or 3 days. I suspect it's the writing rather than the reading that eats battery the fastest, but I have not tested this.

Cloud sync

It has cloud sync. That is, you can get an app for your phone that allows you to add documents to the device and it will copy them over. You can also get a Chrome extension that lets you save web pages to the device. This works quite nicely and the resulting documents are very pleasant to read. However, the sync process seems a bit fragile and sometimes takes a significant amount of time for a shared file or page to appear on the device. There is no way to force it to refresh, which can be a frustration.

Doing more fancy things like syncing your Pocket or Instapaper list to the device is theoretically possible with a lot of faffing about, but I wouldn't count on it. It's not supported officially. Which brings me to...

Hackability

You can ssh to the device and get root. So, in theory you can do pretty much whatever you want. There is also an API for the cloud sync which I think somebody has reverse engineered. There is a community of people writing software of various sorts for it. However, I have found most of it to be pretty immature and not particularly robust or practical. Hopefully this will get better over time. For now, expect a lot of faffery if you want to go far off the beaten path.

Writing

It has the best digital writing experience I am aware of. It has slightly more latency than the iPad under certain circumstances. But in general the texture of the writing surface is far superior to writing on glass. It is easy to forget sometimes that you are not writing on paper.

It does have the ability to convert handwriting to text. In general this works pretty well, but is not perfect.

I got the "plus" stylus, which has an eraser built into it like a pencil. I do not recommend it. It's actually much easier just to use the normal eraser tools built into the software.

Reading

The physical reading experience is really nice. It's very pleasant to read papers and web pages on a large comfortable e-ink display. However, the software for reading is a bit primitive compared with things like the Kindle. Reading PDFs is ok, but the zoom functionality is a bit clunky, and it can be hard to get it to the perfect zoom level. Ebooks are better since they automatically reflow the text to fit.

There is no ability to select words and get definitions, which is a major let-down for any e-reader. You can mark up documents and highlight with the stylus, which is really fun.

Overall the reading experience is good but let down by feature-poor reading software. Hopefully this will improve with time.
